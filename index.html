About Gandhi

Mahatma Gandhi (1869–1948) was the leader of India’s non-violent independence movement against British rule. His life remains a symbol of peace, truth, and resilience.

Mission

Our mission is to educate and inspire people by sharing Gandhi’s life story, values, and philosophy, promoting non-violence, truth, and peace in today’s world.

Vision

We envision a global community where Gandhi’s principles guide people towards justice, harmony, and sustainable living. His teachings remain a beacon of light for future generations.

Early Life and Education

Mahatma Gandhi was born on 2nd October 1869 in Porbandar, Gujarat. He grew up in a deeply religious family where honesty and discipline were highly valued. As a child, Gandhi was shy but curious, developing a strong sense of morality and truthfulness. He completed his schooling in Rajkot and later traveled to London in 1888 to study law at the Inner Temple. During his stay in England, he was exposed to Western ideas but remained deeply connected to his Indian roots and values.

Struggles in South Africa

In 1893, Gandhi went to South Africa to practice law. There he faced racial discrimination, which profoundly impacted him. One famous incident occurred when he was thrown off a train at Pietermaritzburg for refusing to move from the ‘whites only’ compartment. These humiliations transformed Gandhi into a social activist. He organized the Indian community to fight against injustice and developed his philosophy of Satyagraha — nonviolent resistance.

Why it matters:

South Africa was Gandhi’s laboratory—where he refined leadership, community organization, and Satyagraha as a disciplined, ethical political technique.

Role in India’s Freedom Movement

Returning to India in 1915, Gandhi became a central figure in the strugg India Movement. Through peaceful protests, boycotts, and marches like the famous Dandi Salt March in 1930, Gandhi inspired millions of Indians to join the fight against British colonial rule. His leadership helped India gain independence in 1947.

Independence & Partition

  • Gandhi worked tirelessly in Noakhali, Bihar, and Calcutta (1946–47) to quell communal violence, undertaking fasts for peace.
  • India achieved independence on August 15, 1947; Gandhi was not part of the celebrations, focusing instead on communal harmony.
  • He was assassinated on January 30, 1948, in New Delhi by Nathuram Godse

Principles & Beliefs

Gandhi’s philosophy was built on truth (Satya) and non-violence (Ahimsa). He believed that peaceful resistance could bring about meaningful social and political change. Gandhi also emphasized self-reliance, simplicity, and respect for all religions. His ideas continue to influence civil rights leaders and peace movements around the world, including Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela.

Legacy and Inspiration

Even after his assassination in 1948, Gandhi’s legacy endures globally. He is remembered as the ‘Father of the Nation’ in India and as a symbol of peace worldwide. His teachings have inspired countless movements for justice and equality across the globe. Statues, memorials, and institutions in his name keep his memory alive, and his principles remain timeless in addressing modern-day challenges.
